# Demand Alpha Workflow

Use this workflow when the user shares news, product launches, procurement signals, technology breakthroughs, supply-chain changes, earnings-call clues, bottleneck ideas, or asks for small-cap beneficiaries. The workflow converts a market-moving item into a testable hypothesis:

```text
news -> observed demand change -> financial-statement transmission -> small-cap elasticity -> validation path
```

Treat outputs as research hypotheses, not personalized investment advice.

## 1. Separate Narrative From Demand

Ask whether anything has actually changed. Prioritize evidence that already exists:

- customers are adopting, paying, deploying, or expanding;
- enterprises or governments are buying;
- suppliers are shipping, backlogged, capacity-constrained, or raising prices;
- production schedules, lead times, utilization, or procurement timelines changed;
- filings, earnings calls, or customer/supplier sources mention the driver;
- niche ecosystems are becoming crowded enough to create second-order demand.

If demand is not observable, classify the idea as `watchlist_only`.

## 2. Translate Demand Into Financial Statements

Use this structure:

```text
Because X is happening, demand for Y is increasing.
That demand can reach A/B/C companies through revenue line Z.
Company N may matter because its market cap, revenue base, or business purity is small enough for the demand shock to change reported results.
```

Map the mechanism to:

- revenue: units, ASP, customers, usage, take rate, backlog conversion;
- gross margin: mix shift, pricing power, utilization, input costs;
- operating leverage: fixed-cost absorption, sales efficiency, R&D leverage;
- cash flow: working capital, capex, inventory turns, prepayments;
- balance sheet: debt capacity, inventory, receivables, deferred revenue, customer deposits.

## 3. Build the Beneficiary Chain

Separate:

- first-order beneficiaries: directly paid by the demand change;
- second-order beneficiaries: suppliers, distributors, component makers, data/tooling providers;
- third-order beneficiaries: distant thematic links that may be too weak.

Prefer short transmission chains with named revenue mechanisms. Penalize ideas where the beneficiary is real but too far away from the cash flow.

## 4. Prefer Small, Pure, Misclassified Candidates

Rank candidates higher when they have:

- small market cap or low revenue base relative to the demand shock;
- high business purity to the specific demand vector;
- key bottleneck, supply-chain, or scarcity position;
- low current investor attention;
- stale market label that misses the new role;
- financial verification likely within 1-4 quarters.

Use:

```text
alpha elasticity ~= incremental demand impact / current company scale
```

Do not over-prioritize obvious mega-cap winners when the user is seeking demand-alpha analysis. Mega-caps may validate the theme, but smaller pure suppliers often carry more financial-statement elasticity.

## 5. Test Market Misclassification

Ask:

- What does the market currently think this company is?
- What might it be becoming if the demand change persists?
- Is the new label durable and near enough to reported numbers to matter?
- Is the market mislabel real, or is the story already consensus?

Misclassification matters only when relabeling can be validated by numbers, not just narrative.

## 6. Build a 1-4 Quarter Validation Chain

Define what would confirm, weaken, or falsify the thesis within 1-4 quarters. Use:

- revenue growth or guidance revisions;
- gross-margin improvement or mix disclosure;
- backlog, book-to-bill, lead times, order commentary;
- inventory drawdown, channel checks, or customer deployments;
- utilization, capacity expansion, or capex plans;
- ASP changes or pricing commentary;
- customer concentration changes;
- management mentioning the demand driver unprompted;
- competitor, supplier, or customer corroboration.

If the idea cannot be verified within a practical reporting window, mark it as long-horizon/watchlist.

## 7. Score Alpha Strength

Use qualitative buckets or a 1-5 score for each dimension:

| Dimension | Question |
| --- | --- |
| Demand certainty | Is demand already occurring or only imagined? |
| Transmission clarity | Can demand clearly flow to named companies? |
| Business purity | Is exposure direct and meaningful? |
| Market-cap elasticity | Is the company small enough for the impact to matter? |
| Market neglect | Is the market missing or misclassifying it? |
| Verification speed | Can filings/calls verify it in 1-4 quarters? |
| Downside risk | What happens if the thesis is wrong? |

Prioritize high demand certainty, clear transmission, high purity, high elasticity, market neglect, and near-term verification.

## 8. Evidence-Conditioned Position Posture

Frame posture as research guidance, not a personalized recommendation:

| Thesis state | Posture |
| --- | --- |
| Demand seems real but transmission unclear | observe / research queue |
| Demand real, transmission clear, no financial proof yet | watchlist or small exploratory posture if user risk framework allows |
| Financials begin validating and market still underprices | thesis upgraded; monitor add-on evidence |
| Thesis becomes consensus and valuation stretches | lower return expectations; valuation gate |
| Key assumptions are falsified | downgrade, exit research queue, or remove from watchlist |

## Output Requirements

For demand-alpha runs, include:

- primary candidate and why it is most elastic;
- observed demand change;
- financial-statement transmission;
- beneficiary chain and transmission distance;
- market misclassification;
- alpha elasticity;
- 1-4 quarter validation chain;
- falsification conditions;
- evidence gaps and next diligence tasks.
